Reference List: Basic Rules

The reference list, located at the end of your paper, provides full publication information for all in-text citations. This section outlines the essential rules for constructing an accurate and well-formatted reference list.

Fundamental Principles:

  • Start on a New Page: The reference list should begin on a new page after the body of your paper.

    This ensures a clear separation between your main content and the list of sources, enhancing readability and organization. Use a page break if writing in a word processor.

  • Title: Label the page "References" (centered, bold, at the top). Do not italicize or underline.

    The title "References" should be bolded and centered at the top of the page. It should not be enclosed in quotation marks, underlined, or italicized. This clear labeling helps readers immediately identify the section's purpose.

  • Double-Spacing: The entire reference list should be double-spaced.

    Maintain double-spacing throughout all entries in your reference list. This spacing contributes to legibility and aligns with standard academic formatting practices.

  • Hanging Indent: The first line of each reference entry is flush with the left margin, and subsequent lines are indented 0.5 inches (1.27 cm).

    A hanging indent means that the first line of an entry starts at the left margin, while all subsequent lines of that same entry are indented. This visual formatting helps readers quickly scan the list and identify individual entries, as the author's last name is the first visible element of each entry.

  • Alphabetical Order: Entries are organized alphabetically by the first author's last name (or by title if there is no author).

    Arrange all entries in your reference list alphabetically by the surname of the first author. If a source has no author, alphabetize it by the first significant word of its title (ignoring "A," "An," "The"). This systematic order makes it easy for readers to find specific sources.

  • Only Cited Works: Only include sources that you have cited within your paper. Do not include works you read but did not directly use.

    The reference list should exclusively contain entries for sources that you have directly cited in the body of your paper. This distinguishes it from a bibliography, which might include all sources consulted.

  • Consistency: Maintain consistent formatting for all elements within each entry (e.g., author names, publication dates, titles, journal names, DOIs).

    Strict adherence to the chosen citation style's guidelines for every element—such as capitalization, italics, punctuation, and the order of information—is paramount. Consistency ensures professionalism and accuracy across your entire list.

Common Elements in a Reference Entry (General Order):

  1. Author(s)

    This is the individual or group responsible for the work. It is the primary element used for alphabetical sorting.

  2. Date of publication

    Indicates when the work was published, typically in parentheses. For articles and web pages, it may include month and day.

  3. Title of work

    The full title of the article, book, chapter, or other resource. It may be italicized depending on the source type.

  4. Source information (e.g., journal title, publisher, URL, DOI)

    This provides details about where the work was published or retrieved from, helping readers locate the original source.

Adhering to these basic rules ensures your reference list is clear, comprehensive, and easy for readers to navigate to your original sources.
