Reference List: Books

Books are a foundational source of academic information. This section provides guidelines for citing entire books, specific chapters within edited books, and different editions of books.

Entire Book:

  • Format: Author, A. A. (Year). Title of work (Edition, if applicable). Publisher.

    For a standalone book, list the author(s), publication year, italicized title of the book, and the publisher. If there's an edition number (e.g., 2nd ed.), include it in parentheses after the title.

  • Example: Johnson, R. L. (2020). The art of academic research. University Press.

    This is a straightforward example for a book with a single author and a known publisher.

  • For an edited book without chapter authors, list the editor(s) as author(s) followed by (Ed.) or (Eds.).

    If the entire book is a collection of works by various authors, and you are citing the whole collection, list the editor(s) in the author position, followed by "(Ed.)" or "(Eds.)" in parentheses.

Chapter in an Edited Book:

  • Format: Author, A. A. (Year). Title of chapter. In E. E. Editor (Ed.), Title of edited book (pp. pages). Publisher.

    When citing a specific chapter from a book that has different authors for different chapters and one or more editors for the whole book, you cite the chapter author first, then the chapter title, followed by information about the edited book (editor's name, book title, and page range of the chapter).

  • Example: Smith, P. R. (2019). Digital literacy in the 21st century. In M. L. Green (Ed.), Essays on modern education (pp. 75-90). Academic Press.

    This example demonstrates how to format a chapter reference, including the chapter's author and title, followed by the editor(s) and title of the larger edited volume, along with the specific page numbers of the chapter.

Book with a DOI or from a database:

  • If a book has a DOI, include it at the end of the reference.

    A DOI is a unique identifier. If a book, whether print or electronic, has a DOI, always include it as a URL at the end of the reference. This provides a stable link to the content.

  • Format: Author, A. A. (Year). Title of work. Publisher. DOI

    Example: Davis, K. (2021). Understanding historical narratives. Cambridge University Press. https://doi.org/10.1017/9781108999999

  • If a book is obtained from an academic database but does not have a DOI, do not include a URL or database name. Treat it as a print book.

    For books retrieved from commonly used academic research databases (like those from university libraries) that do not have a DOI, APA style generally recommends treating them as if they were print books. This means you do not need to include retrieval information (like a URL or database name), as these databases are assumed to be widely available.

Ebook without DOI:

  • Format: Author, A. A. (Year). Title of work. Publisher.

    If an e-book does not have a DOI and is not from a specialized academic research database (e.g., a book from a public library's e-book collection), you can cite it like a print book. No URL is needed unless the source is difficult to locate.

  • Example: Brown, L. (2022). Introduction to data science. Data Books.

    This illustrates the straightforward citation for an e-book without a DOI, similar to a physical book.

Accurate book citations are essential for acknowledging scholarship and allowing readers to access original texts.
