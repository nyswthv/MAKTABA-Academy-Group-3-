In-text Citations: The Basics

In-text citations are a crucial part of academic writing, acknowledging the sources of information you use within your paper. This section covers the basic principles of how to incorporate these citations correctly.

Purpose of In-text Citations:

  • To give credit to the original author or source of ideas, information, or direct quotes.

    Proper attribution prevents plagiarism and acknowledges the intellectual property of others, which is fundamental to academic integrity.

  • To allow readers to easily locate the full source in your reference list.

    Each in-text citation acts as a signpost, guiding your readers to the corresponding full entry in your reference list at the end of the paper, where they can find complete publication details.

  • To avoid plagiarism and uphold academic integrity.

    Plagiarism, whether intentional or accidental, involves presenting someone else's work or ideas as your own. Accurate in-text citation is your primary tool to demonstrate that you are engaging with existing scholarship ethically.

General Rules:

  • Typically includes the author's last name and the year of publication.

    The most common elements for an in-text citation are the author's surname and the year the work was published. For example, (Smith, 2020).

  • For direct quotes, also include the page number (or paragraph number for online sources).

    When you use a direct quotation, you must provide the exact location of the quote in the source. For print sources, this is usually a page number (p. 25). For online sources without page numbers, use a paragraph number (para. 3) or a section heading if available.

  • Place the citation as close as possible to the borrowed material, usually at the end of the sentence before the period.

    The citation should immediately follow the information it supports. If citing at the end of a sentence, place the citation before the final punctuation mark. Example: "The research highlighted significant findings" (Johnson, 2018, p. 45).

  • There are two main ways to integrate citations:
    1. Parenthetical Citation: The author's last name and year of publication appear in parentheses at the end of the sentence.

      Example: "The study showed significant results (Smith, 2020)." This method is concise and commonly used when the focus is on the information rather than the author.

    2. Narrative Citation: The author's name is incorporated into the sentence, followed by the year in parentheses.

      Example: "According to Smith (2020), the study showed significant results." This method emphasizes the author and their contribution to the topic.

Understanding these basics will help you properly integrate evidence into your writing while giving appropriate credit to your sources.
