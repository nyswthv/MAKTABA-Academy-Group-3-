Reference List: Other Non-Print Sources

This section provides guidance for citing sources that are not typically printed or easily categorized, such as personal communications, interviews, and live performances. Many of these sources are typically cited *only* in-text as they are not recoverable by others.

Personal Communication (e.g., email, private interview, direct message, memo, letter, phone call):

  • Personal communications are not included in the reference list because they do not provide recoverable data. Cite them only in-text.

    These types of communications are generally not accessible to other researchers, so they should only be cited in the text of your paper. This means they will not appear in your reference list.

  • In-text Format: (A. Author, personal communication, Month Day, Year)

    Example: (J. Smith, personal communication, April 15, 2024). This format provides enough information for context without requiring a full reference list entry.

Lectures, Speeches, or Classroom Materials:

  • If the material is not recorded or available publicly, it's typically treated as a personal communication and cited only in-text.

    For live lectures, discussions, or unrecorded classroom materials, treat them like personal communications. The key is whether another researcher could access the exact same content you are citing.

  • If the lecture or material is recorded and publicly accessible (e.g., on a university website or YouTube), treat it as an audiovisual source or webpage.

    If the material is recorded and publicly available (e.g., a lecture video on a university's YouTube channel, a publicly accessible course document), then you should cite it fully in your reference list using the appropriate format (e.g., for a video or webpage).

  • Example (In-text only if not recoverable): (Professor Doe, class lecture, March 20, 2024)

    This format is used when the lecture is not publicly recorded or distributed, and thus cannot be retrieved by others.

Artwork in a Museum or Gallery:

  • Format: Artist, A. A. (Year). Title of artwork [Description of medium]. Museum, City, State, Country.

    Cite the artist, the year the artwork was created, the italicized title of the artwork, a bracketed description of the medium (e.g., [Painting], [Sculpture]), followed by the name of the museum or gallery, and its location.

  • Example: Van Gogh, V. (1889). The starry night [Painting]. The Museum of Modern Art, New York, NY, United States.

    This demonstrates a standard citation for a physical piece of artwork in a museum.

Data from a Research Instrument (e.g., survey data you collected):

  • This is typically not cited in the reference list unless you are referring to a publicly archived dataset. Your own raw data is usually discussed in the methodology section.

    If you have collected your own data (e.g., from a survey or experiment), you generally do not include it in the reference list. Instead, describe your data collection methods thoroughly in your paper's methodology section. If you are using a publicly available, archived dataset (e.g., from a data repository), you would cite it as a dataset.

When in doubt about a non-print source, prioritize providing enough information for your reader to understand the origin, and consider if the source is recoverable by others. If not, an in-text citation might be sufficient.
